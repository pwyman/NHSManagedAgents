# FOI Exemptions Reference Guide — NHS Westshire ICB

> **Purpose:** This guide is used by the FOI Agent to identify applicable exemptions when drafting FOI responses. It covers the main FOIA 2000 exemptions relevant to an NHS ICB, the Environmental Information Regulations 2004, and the distinction between the two regimes. All final exemption decisions require approval by the FOI Lead (Diane Okafor). Refusals must also go to the FOI Lead before sending.

---

## Part 1: Understanding the Two Regimes

### When does FOIA apply?

The Freedom of Information Act 2000 applies to all recorded information held by the ICB, except where an exemption applies. "Recorded information" means information held in any form — documents, emails, databases, spreadsheets, photographs, audio recordings, etc. — whether in digital or paper form.

### When does EIR apply instead of FOIA?

The Environmental Information Regulations 2004 (EIR) apply instead of FOIA where the requested information relates to **environmental information**. EIR has a wider definition of environmental information than many requesters expect. Environmental information includes information on:
- The state of the air, water, soil, land, landscape, biological diversity, and natural sites
- Emissions, discharges, and other releases into the environment
- Energy, noise, radiation, and waste
- **Measures and policies affecting or likely to affect environmental elements** (this is where NHS ICBs are most commonly caught — e.g. information about planning decisions for health centres, air quality at ICB premises, estate sustainability plans)
- Reports on the implementation of environmental legislation
- Cost-benefit and economic analysis used in connection with any of the above

**Key EIR differences from FOIA:**
- EIR uses "exceptions" not "exemptions"; all exceptions (except reg.12(5)(a) international relations) are subject to a public interest test
- There is no cost limit exception (unlike FOIA s.12)
- The EIR right is to information "on request" — the authority must actively assist the requester
- EIR requests can be made verbally (FOI requires written requests)
- The duty to confirm or deny applies under both regimes

**When in doubt about FOIA vs EIR:** Apply EIR. It is better to apply the correct regime and explain this to the requester than to refuse under FOIA when EIR should have been applied (a common ICO criticism).

## Part 2: FOIA Exemptions — NHS ICB Reference

### Structure of exemptions

FOIA exemptions are either:
- **Absolute**: the authority can refuse without a public interest test. The exemption applies if the condition is met.
- **Qualified**: the authority must first establish that the exemption applies, and then apply a **public interest test (PIT)** — weighing the public interest in disclosure against the public interest in maintaining the exemption. The authority must disclose unless the balance of public interest favours withholding.

---

### Section 21 — Information Accessible by Other Means (Absolute)

**What it covers:** Information that is already reasonably accessible to the applicant, whether or not on payment.

**When it applies in the NHS context:**
- ICB Annual Report and Accounts (published on website and on Companies House)
- Board meeting minutes and papers (published on website)
- Gender Pay Gap Report (published on gov.uk)
- NHS Staff Survey results (published at nhsstaffsurveys.com)
- Register of Declared Interests (published on ICB website)
- Integrated Care Strategy and Joint Forward Plan (published on website)
- Contracts above £25,000 (published on Contracts Finder)
- Monthly spend data (published on transparency pages)
- CQC reports on ICB (published on cqc.org.uk)
- NHS publication scheme documents

**How to apply:** State clearly where the information is available. Provide the URL or specific location. Do not make the requester search — direct them precisely.

**Note:** The information must actually be accessible, not just theoretically available. If a website link is broken or the document has been archived and is hard to find, s.21 may not apply — check before relying on it.

**Draft refusal notice wording:**
> "The information you have requested falls within the scope of Section 21 of the Freedom of Information Act 2000 (information accessible by other means). This exemption is absolute and no public interest test is required. The information is available at: [URL / publication]. If you have difficulty accessing this information, please contact us and we will provide a copy."

---

### Section 22 — Information Intended for Future Publication (Qualified)

**What it covers:** Information that the authority intends to publish at some future date, where it is reasonable to withhold it until publication.

**When it applies in the NHS context:**
- Board papers and reports that have been finalised but not yet presented at a public Board meeting
- Annual Report and Accounts during the audit/publication process (typically April–June each year)
- Procurement award notices before the standstill period has elapsed
- Strategy documents under consultation prior to publication

**Public interest test considerations:**
- Favour disclosure: the sooner information is public, the more useful it is to democratic accountability
- Favour withholding: premature publication could interfere with Board deliberation, or undermine the procurement process

**Note:** There must be a genuine, settled intention to publish by a specific (or reasonably foreseeable) date. The ICB cannot rely on s.22 indefinitely. Always state when the information will be published.

---

### Section 31 — Law Enforcement (Qualified)

**What it covers:** Information whose disclosure would or would be likely to prejudice the prevention or detection of crime, the apprehension or prosecution of offenders, or various other law enforcement functions.

**When it applies in the NHS context (rarely):**
- Very rarely applicable to an NHS ICB
- Could apply if the ICB is aware of fraud investigations by NHS Counter Fraud Authority (NHSCFA) and disclosure would prejudice the investigation
- Could apply to information about specific safeguarding referrals where disclosure could identify a perpetrator and prejudice proceedings

**Public interest test:** Given the narrow applicability to NHS ICBs, the public interest in disclosure will frequently outweigh the interest in maintaining this exemption unless there is a specific, concrete risk.

---

### Section 36 — Prejudice to Effective Conduct of Public Affairs (Qualified)

**What it covers:** Information whose disclosure would or would be likely to: (a) prejudice the maintenance of the convention of collective responsibility of ministers; (b) inhibit the free and frank provision of advice; (c) inhibit the free and frank exchange of views for the purposes of deliberation; or (d) otherwise prejudice, or would be likely to otherwise prejudice, the effective conduct of public affairs.

**Special requirement:** For this exemption to apply to a public authority other than a government department, it must be shown that the reasonable opinion of the "qualified person" (in an NHS ICB, this is the Board Chair or CEO) is that disclosure would have the prejudicial effect. The FOI Lead must arrange for this opinion to be obtained — this cannot be done autonomously.

**When it applies in the NHS context:**
- Draft reports, discussion papers, and internal policy options papers where free and frank deliberation is needed
- Early-stage business cases before Board approval
- Feedback from staff in internal consultations, where publication would discourage future frankness
- Sensitive strategic deliberations about provider performance

**Public interest test:** FOIA was designed to open government. The ICO expects a genuinely high bar for s.36. The mere fact that information is in draft form is not sufficient. There must be a plausible, specific prejudice.

**Note:** Always escalate s.36 to the FOI Lead. The qualified person's opinion must be documented.

---

### Section 38 — Health and Safety (Qualified)

**What it covers:** Information whose disclosure would or would be likely to endanger the physical or mental health or the safety of any individual.

**When it applies in the NHS context:**
- Very rarely applicable to most ICB information
- Could apply to detailed information about security arrangements at ICB premises that could be exploited
- Could apply to information about the location of vulnerable patients in specific circumstances
- Could apply to information about staff under threat

**Public interest test:** The public interest in health and safety (preventing harm) will usually weigh heavily in favour of withholding. However, the risk must be genuine and specific, not merely theoretical.

---

### Section 40 — Personal Data (Mixed — Absolute for Third Parties; Qualified for Applicant's Own Data)

**What it covers:** Information that constitutes the personal data of individuals, where disclosure under FOIA would breach UK GDPR data protection principles.

**This is the most frequently applied exemption in NHS organisations.**

**Two conditions:**
- **s.40(2) — Third-party personal data (ABSOLUTE):** Where the information relates to an identifiable living third party (i.e. not the applicant) and disclosure would breach UK GDPR. No public interest test is required.
- **s.40(1) — Applicant's own data (NOT an exemption — redirect to SAR):** Where the applicant is asking for their own personal data, this is a Subject Access Request under UK GDPR, not an FOI request.

**When s.40(2) applies in the NHS context:**
- Salary details of any individual employee (even senior managers, unless disclosed in Annual Report per statutory requirements)
- Individual sickness absence records
- Individual disciplinary or grievance outcomes (where the individual is identifiable)
- Information that, even if anonymised, could be used to identify an individual in context
- Patient-level data (almost always — noting the ICB holds limited clinical data)
- Home address or personal contact details of any individual
- Information about a named individual's employment history

**Key principles from UK GDPR Article 5 to apply:**
- **Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency** — is there a legal basis for disclosure?
- **Purpose limitation** — the data was not collected for the purpose of FOI disclosure
- **Data minimisation** — disclose only what is necessary
- **Accuracy** — ensure disclosed data is accurate

**NHS-specific note on salary disclosure:**
- AfC pay band ranges are publicly available and can be disclosed
- Individual salaries of named staff are not disclosed (personal data)
- VSM (Very Senior Manager) and board member pay is disclosed in bands in the Annual Report per NHS statutory requirements — this is lawful because it is required by law

**Practical approach:** Where information contains third-party personal data, consider whether redaction is possible to allow partial disclosure of the non-personal elements. Do not refuse the entire request if only part of it contains personal data.

**When in doubt about s.40:** Copy Andrew Kellaway (DPO) immediately.

### Section 41 — Confidentiality (Absolute)

**What it covers:** Information obtained by the public authority from any other person where disclosure would constitute a breach of confidence actionable by that other person.

**Two conditions must both be met:**
1. The information must have been obtained from another person (not generated internally)
2. Disclosure would constitute an actionable breach of confidence (i.e. the information has the necessary quality of confidence, it was communicated in circumstances importing an obligation of confidence, and there would be unauthorised use)

**When it applies in the NHS context:**
- Provider-to-ICB correspondence marked as confidential and provided in confidence (e.g. detailed operational plans)
- Third-party financial information provided during procurement (tender submissions)
- Legal advice provided by external solicitors (though note s.42 — Legal Professional Privilege — may be the more appropriate exemption for legal advice)
- Patient information shared with the ICB by providers
- Commercially sensitive information provided by contractors

**Note:** The fact that information is marked "confidential" by the sender is not determinative — the ICB must assess whether an action for breach of confidence would actually succeed. Many documents marked confidential would not meet the legal test. Conversely, some confidential information is not marked as such but clearly carries the obligation.

**Absolute exemption:** If s.41 applies, the authority can refuse without a public interest test. However, the ICO has noted that even absolute exemptions should be applied carefully.

---

### Section 43 — Commercial Interests (Qualified)

**What it covers:** Information whose disclosure would or would be likely to prejudice: (a) the commercial interests of any person; or (b) the ability of any public authority to conduct commercial activities.

**When it applies in the NHS context:**
- Specific unit prices within procurement contracts where disclosure would give competitors or other parties an unfair advantage
- Tender evaluation scores and commercial assessments for ongoing or future procurement
- Commercially sensitive financial modelling prepared in contemplation of negotiations
- Market-sensitive information about planned procurement

**When it does NOT apply:**
- The mere existence of a contract (the fact the ICB has a contract with provider X is not commercially sensitive)
- General contract values already published on Contracts Finder
- Service specifications that describe what is being commissioned but do not reveal pricing strategy
- Aggregate spend figures (usually)

**Public interest test considerations:**
- Favour disclosure: the ICB is spending public money; transparency in public procurement is a strong public interest; accountability for commissioning decisions
- Favour withholding: preventing prejudice to future procurement exercises; protecting the ICB's negotiating position; protecting third-party commercial interests in accordance with confidentiality obligations

**Always escalate to the FOI Lead and copy the CFO** when s.43 is being considered. The CFO is best placed to assess commercial sensitivity.

---

### Section 12 — Cost Limit (Not an Exemption — a Fee/Cost Provision)

**What it covers:** The authority is not obliged to comply with a request if the cost of compliance exceeds the "appropriate limit" — for public authorities other than central government: £450 (equivalent to 18 hours of work at £25/hour).

**How to calculate:** Only costs of finding, retrieving, and extracting the information count. Reading, considering, consulting, and redacting do NOT count towards the limit.

**What to do when s.12 applies:**
- Issue a refusal notice explaining the cost limit
- Offer to assist the requester to refine their request to one that can be complied with within the limit
- If appropriate, explain what information the ICB can provide within the limit

**Important:** Do not apply s.12 to avoid answering a difficult question. The ICO scrutinises s.12 refusals carefully.

**Escalation:** All s.12 decisions must be approved by the FOI Lead.

---

### Section 14 — Vexatious Requests (Not an Exemption — a Section in its Own Right)

**What it covers:** The authority is not obliged to comply with a request that is vexatious. A request may be vexatious if it: imposes a significant burden on the authority; is designed to cause disruption or annoyance; lacks any serious purpose; or is part of a pattern of obsessive or manifestly unreasonable requests.

**Key ICO guidance factors:**
- Whether the request would impose a significant burden
- Whether the request has a serious purpose or value
- Whether the request is harassing or represents a pattern of abuse
- Whether the request is made in bad faith

**Note:** s.14 is a high bar. The ICO is critical of authorities that apply it too readily. It should not be used to deflect uncomfortable but legitimate requests.

**Always escalate to the FOI Lead immediately** before any s.14 reliance.

## Part 3: The Public Interest Test (PIT)

For all qualified exemptions, where the exemption is established, the authority must apply the public interest test. The authority must disclose unless the public interest in maintaining the exemption outweighs the public interest in disclosure.

**Factors favouring disclosure:**
- Promoting accountability and transparency for public bodies
- Allowing individuals and organisations to understand decisions that affect them
- Enabling the public to scrutinise the use of public funds
- Contributing to debate on matters of public concern
- Disclosing potential wrongdoing or mismanagement

**Factors favouring withholding:**
- Prevention of harm (physical, commercial, public safety)
- Maintaining effective working of public services
- Protecting the free flow of advice and deliberation within organisations
- Protecting the privacy and dignity of individuals
- Maintaining legitimate commercial confidence

**Timing:** The public interest test is applied at the date of the authority's response, not the date of the request. If circumstances change (e.g. a procurement concludes), the balance may shift.

**Drafting the PIT:** When preparing a PIT argument for the FOI Lead, present both sides balanced against each other. Do not write a one-sided argument. The FOI Lead will make the final decision.

## Part 4: EIR Exceptions (Environmental Information Regulations 2004)

EIR exceptions are in regulations 12 and 13. Unlike FOIA absolute exemptions, **all EIR exceptions are subject to a public interest test** (with the narrow exception of reg.12(5)(a) — adverse effect on international relations, national defence, etc.).

### Key EIR exceptions for NHS ICBs:

| EIR Regulation | Exception | NHS Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| reg.12(4)(a) | Information not held | As with FOIA — not held |
| reg.12(4)(b) | Manifestly unreasonable request | Equivalent to s.12 cost limit under FOIA |
| reg.12(4)(c) | Request too general | Can request clarification |
| reg.12(4)(e) | Internal communications | Internal deliberation — equivalent to s.36 in some respects |
| reg.12(5)(b) | Adverse effect on course of justice | Equivalent to s.31/s.42 in some respects |
| reg.12(5)(d) | Adverse effect on confidentiality of proceedings | Rare |
| reg.12(5)(e) | Adverse effect on confidentiality of commercial/industrial information | Equivalent to s.43 |
| reg.12(5)(f) | Adverse effect on interests of a person providing information voluntarily | Confidentiality similar to s.41 |
| reg.12(5)(g) | Adverse effect on environment | Environmental protective — rare for NHS |
| reg.13 | Personal data | Equivalent to s.40 (personal data) |

**EIR note:** The duty to confirm or deny under EIR is slightly different from FOIA. Under EIR, the authority must actively help the requester — a pure "not held" response should include advice on where to find the information.

## Part 5: Section 17 — Refusal Notices

Every refusal of a FOI request must include a refusal notice (s.17) that:

1. States the exemption(s) relied upon
2. Specifies (for qualified exemptions) that the exemption is qualified
3. Sets out the public interest test conclusion (for qualified exemptions)
4. **Always includes the requester's rights:**
   - Right to request an internal review (within 40 working days of the response)
   - Right to complain to the ICO (at the conclusion of or instead of the internal review)
